## Undergraduate Research

For my bachelor thesis in the [Department of Electrical and Electronic Engineering at Kyoto University](https://www.s-ee.t.kyoto-u.ac.jp/ja/index.html), I conducted an independent research project titled *Computational Modeling of Decision-Making in Self-Learning and Observational Learning*. This work was conducted in the academic environment of [Ishii Lab](https://ishiilab.jp/jp/members.html). The project examined how people integrate information from their own direct experience and from observed actions of others during decision-making.

The study used a bandit learning task with Self, Other, Report, and Choice blocks. Participants learned reward values through their own choices in the Self condition and inferred reward values from observed action sequences in the Other condition. The task also included pseudo-feedback about learning performance to examine how feedback-related bias influenced reward estimation and later choices.

The project combined behavioral experiment design and computational modeling. The analysis included:

- reward estimation from behavioral reports
- modeling observational learning with Maximum Entropy Inverse Reinforcement Learning
- analysis of pseudo-feedback effects on decision-making
- clustering of participants based on reward-estimation errors
- clustering of participants using IRL model parameters
- analysis of individual differences in observational learning

In the behavioral data, participants' choice performance was above chance, and sessions with more accurate reward estimation showed better choice performance. The analysis also suggested that pseudo-feedback affected reward-estimation bias, especially in the self-learning condition, while the observational-learning condition showed larger individual differences. Clustering based on reward-estimation errors and clustering based on IRL model parameters both suggested meaningful individual differences in observational learning.

This project strengthened my interest in using computational models to understand human learning strategies, decision-making biases, and individual differences.
